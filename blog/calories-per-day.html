How Many Calories Should You Eat Daily?

Understanding your calorie needs based on age, gender, and how active you are.

Calories are the units of energy your body uses to perform every function — from breathing and pumping blood to walking, exercising, and thinking. Every day, your body requires a specific number of calories just to keep you alive, plus additional calories to fuel your physical activity. Understanding how many calories you should eat daily is one of the most important foundations of good nutrition.

What Is a Calorie?

A calorie (technically a kilocalorie, or kcal) is a measurement of energy. When you eat food, your body converts its chemical energy into fuel. This fuel powers everything from your heartbeat and brain activity to physical movement. Different macronutrients provide different amounts of energy per gram: protein provides 4 kcal/g, carbohydrates provide 4 kcal/g, and fat provides 9 kcal/g. Alcohol provides 7 kcal/g, which is why alcoholic drinks can contribute significantly to daily calorie intake without providing nutritional value.

How Many Calories Does the Average Adult Need?

Calorie needs vary significantly between individuals, but general guidelines provide a useful starting point. Most health authorities base their recommendations on a sedentary to moderately active lifestyle:

What Determines Your Daily Calorie Needs?

Your total daily calorie requirement — known as Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) — is made up of several components:

Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR)

BMR is the number of calories your body burns at complete rest to maintain vital functions such as breathing, circulation, cell production, and temperature regulation. BMR typically accounts for 60–75% of total daily energy expenditure. It is influenced by body size, muscle mass, age, and sex. People with more muscle mass have a higher BMR because muscle tissue burns more calories at rest than fat tissue.

Physical Activity Level

This is the most variable component of your calorie needs. A sedentary person who sits at a desk all day burns far fewer calories than a construction worker or professional athlete. Physical activity is typically categorised into 5 levels, each assigned a multiplier that is applied to your BMR to give your TDEE. Even light activity like a 30-minute daily walk can add 200–300 kcal to your daily burn.

Thermic Effect of Food (TEF)

Digesting, absorbing, and processing food requires energy. This is called the thermic effect of food, and it accounts for roughly 10% of total daily calorie expenditure. Protein has the highest thermic effect (20–30% of its calories are burned during digestion), followed by carbohydrates (5–10%) and fat (0–3%). This is one reason why high-protein diets are associated with slightly greater calorie burning.

Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis (NEAT)

NEAT includes all movement that is not deliberate exercise — fidgeting, standing, walking around the house, gesturing while talking. Research shows that NEAT can vary by up to 2,000 kcal per day between individuals, making it a surprisingly significant factor in total calorie expenditure. People with high NEAT naturally burn more calories without formal exercise.

Calorie Needs for Different Goals

Weight Loss

To lose weight, you need to consume fewer calories than your body burns — a calorie deficit. A deficit of approximately 500 kcal per day typically results in around 0.5 kg (1 lb) of weight loss per week, which is considered a safe and sustainable rate for most people. Very low calorie diets (below 1,200 kcal for women or 1,500 kcal for men) are not recommended without medical supervision as they can lead to muscle loss, nutrient deficiencies, and metabolic adaptation. Use our calorie deficit calculator to find your personal target.

Muscle Gain

To build muscle effectively, you need to consume slightly more calories than your body burns — a calorie surplus. A moderate surplus of 200–500 kcal per day, combined with progressive resistance training, provides enough energy for muscle protein synthesis without excessive fat gain. Our calorie surplus calculator helps you find the right target based on your goals.

Weight Maintenance

Eating at your TDEE — your total daily energy expenditure — maintains your current weight. This requires tracking your intake and adjusting based on whether your weight is trending up or down over time. Small daily variations of ±100–200 kcal are perfectly normal and do not require immediate adjustment.

Calorie Quality vs Calorie Quantity

While calorie balance determines whether you gain, lose, or maintain weight, the quality of those calories significantly affects your health, energy levels, and body composition. 2,000 calories of whole foods — lean protein, vegetables, fruits, whole grains — provides a very different nutritional outcome to 2,000 calories of ultra-processed food, even though the calorie number is identical. High-quality calories provide vitamins, minerals, fibre, and essential fatty acids that support every system in the body.

Prioritising protein is particularly important. Protein is the most satiating macronutrient and has the highest thermic effect. Eating adequate protein — typically 1.6–2.4g per kg of body weight — helps preserve muscle mass during weight loss and supports muscle building during a surplus. Practical Tips for Managing Daily Calories

You do not need to count every calorie to manage your intake effectively. A few evidence-based strategies make it much easier to stay within your target range. Eating whole, minimally processed foods reduces calorie density while increasing nutrient density. Including protein and vegetables in every meal promotes satiety and reduces overeating. Tracking your food intake — even for just a few weeks — builds awareness of portion sizes and calorie content that stays with you long-term.
