What Is BMI and Why It Matters

Understanding body mass index and what it really tells you about your health.

Body Mass Index (BMI) is one of the most commonly used health measurements in the world. Doctors, researchers, and health professionals use BMI as a quick way to evaluate whether a person has a healthy body weight relative to their height. While BMI does not directly measure body fat, it provides a useful estimate that helps identify potential health risks. Understanding BMI can help individuals make better decisions about nutrition, exercise, and overall lifestyle habits.

What Is BMI?

BMI stands for Body Mass Index. It is a numerical value derived from a person's weight and height. The concept was developed in the 19th century by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet and was originally called the Quetelet Index. It was later renamed Body Mass Index and adopted by health organisations worldwide as a standard screening tool.

BMI = weight (kg) ÷ height² (m)

For example, if someone weighs 70 kg and is 1.75 metres tall, their BMI is 70 ÷ (1.75 × 1.75) = 22.9 — placing them in the healthy weight category. You can check your own result with our free BMI Calculator, which also supports pounds and feet.

BMI Categories

The World Health Organization classifies BMI into the following ranges for adults:

Why BMI Matters

BMI is used because it gives clinicians and individuals a fast, cost-free way to assess weight-related health risks. Despite being a simple calculation, it has been validated in thousands of research studies. People with a BMI above 25 are statistically more likely to develop type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, high blood pressure, stroke, obstructive sleep apnea, and certain cancers including breast, colon and endometrial cancer.

A BMI below 18.5 carries its own risks: malnutrition, weakened immune function, anaemia, osteoporosis, and fertility problems. Both extremes of the BMI spectrum warrant attention from a healthcare professional.

Monitoring Changes Over Time

BMI is a useful tool for monitoring long-term changes in body weight. When someone begins a new diet or exercise programme, tracking BMI over months helps them see whether progress is being made. Research shows that even a 5–10% reduction in body weight can significantly improve blood pressure, blood sugar levels, and cholesterol — all without reaching the healthy BMI range first.

Clinical and Public Health Use

In clinical settings, BMI is used as an initial screening step when assessing a patient's weight. A BMI above 30 may prompt a doctor to recommend lifestyle changes or specialist referral. Bariatric surgery is typically considered at BMI above 40, or above 35 with serious health complications. At a population level, BMI data informs government health policy, school nutrition programmes, and public health funding decisions worldwide.

Limitations of BMI

BMI cannot distinguish between different types of body mass — it does not differentiate between fat, muscle, bone, and water. A professional athlete with high muscle mass may have a BMI in the overweight range despite having very low body fat. Conversely, an older adult with normal BMI may carry excess body fat due to age-related muscle loss (sarcopenic obesity), which BMI would miss entirely.

BMI also ignores fat distribution. Visceral fat — stored around internal organs in the abdominal area — carries far greater health risks than subcutaneous fat just under the skin. Two people with identical BMIs can have very different amounts of visceral fat and very different health risk profiles. For this reason, BMI works best alongside other measurements such as body fat percentage, waist-to-height ratio, and blood test results.

BMI Across Different Populations

Standard BMI thresholds were developed based on data from European populations and do not apply equally to all ethnic groups. Research shows that people of South Asian, East Asian, and Southeast Asian descent tend to carry more body fat at lower BMI values. Many Asian health authorities therefore use lower thresholds — 23 for overweight and 27.5 for obesity — for their populations.

For children and teenagers, adult BMI charts are not appropriate. Children's BMI must be interpreted using age- and sex-specific percentile charts, as their body composition changes significantly during growth. Our children's BMI calculator provides a more suitable assessment for younger users.
