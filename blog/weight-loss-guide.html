How to Lose Weight — A Science-Based Guide

Everything you need to know about calorie deficits, metabolism, and sustainable fat loss.

Weight loss is one of the most searched health topics in the world, yet it is also one of the most misunderstood. Fad diets, conflicting advice, and misleading marketing make it difficult to know where to start. The good news is that the science of weight loss is actually quite straightforward. This guide explains how it works and gives you the tools to approach it sustainably.

The Fundamental Principle: Calorie Balance

At its most basic level, weight loss comes down to one principle: you must consume fewer calories than your body expends. This is called a calorie deficit. When you are in a calorie deficit, your body draws on stored energy — primarily body fat — to meet its energy needs, causing you to lose weight over time.

Conversely, a calorie surplus — consuming more than you burn — causes weight gain. Weight maintenance requires calorie balance. This principle, known as the First Law of Thermodynamics applied to human metabolism, is supported by decades of research and applies regardless of the specific diet approach you follow.

A deficit of approximately 500 kcal per day typically produces around 0.5 kg (1 lb) of weight loss per week. A deficit of 1,000 kcal per day produces approximately 1 kg per week. Deficits larger than this are generally not recommended as they increase the risk of muscle loss, nutritional deficiencies, and metabolic adaptation. Understanding Your Metabolism

Your metabolism — specifically your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) — is the total number of calories your body burns in a day. It has four main components: Basal Metabolic Rate (the calories burned at complete rest), the thermic effect of food (calories burned during digestion), Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis or NEAT (calories burned through incidental movement), and deliberate exercise.

Your TDEE is the number you need to eat below to lose weight. It is influenced by your age, sex, height, weight, and activity level. Calculate yours with our TDEE calculator to establish your starting point before setting a calorie target.

Metabolic Adaptation

One important phenomenon that affects long-term weight loss is metabolic adaptation — the body's tendency to reduce its metabolic rate in response to sustained calorie restriction. As you lose weight, your body becomes lighter and therefore requires fewer calories to function. Additionally, the body reduces non-essential energy expenditure (including NEAT) as a survival response to caloric restriction. This is why weight loss often slows after the initial weeks despite no change in diet or exercise. Recalculate your TDEE every 4–6 weeks as your body weight changes to keep your calorie target accurate.

The Role of Protein in Weight Loss

Of all the dietary factors that influence weight loss success, protein intake is arguably the most important. Protein is the most satiating macronutrient — it reduces appetite more effectively than carbohydrates or fat, helping you stick to a calorie deficit without constant hunger. It also has the highest thermic effect of food, meaning approximately 20–30% of the calories in protein are burned during digestion. Most importantly, adequate protein intake (1.6–2.4g per kg of body weight) preserves muscle mass during a calorie deficit, ensuring that weight lost comes from fat rather than muscle tissue. Exercise for Weight Loss

Exercise contributes to weight loss primarily by increasing total calorie expenditure, though its effect is often smaller than people expect. A typical 45-minute moderate-intensity run burns approximately 400–600 kcal depending on body weight — equivalent to a single large meal. This does not diminish the value of exercise, but it does illustrate why diet is generally considered more important than exercise for weight loss, while exercise is more important for weight maintenance and overall health.

Resistance training (weightlifting, bodyweight exercises) is particularly valuable during weight loss because it preserves and builds muscle mass. More muscle mass means a higher resting metabolic rate, making it easier to maintain a calorie deficit over time. A combination of 2–4 resistance training sessions and 2–3 cardio sessions per week is an effective approach for most people.

Setting a Realistic Timeline

Safe and sustainable fat loss occurs at a rate of 0.5–1 kg per week for most people. Faster weight loss is possible but increases the risk of losing muscle, developing nutritional deficiencies, and triggering metabolic adaptation that makes long-term maintenance harder. Patience is the most underrated component of successful weight loss. Even at 0.5 kg per week, someone who commits to a sustainable approach will lose 25 kg in a year — a life-changing amount.

To stay on track, focus on building habits rather than chasing a number on the scale. Body weight fluctuates by 1–3 kg day-to-day due to water retention, food volume, and hormonal changes. Weigh yourself consistently (same time, same conditions) and track the weekly average rather than reacting to daily fluctuations.
