Healthy Body Fat Percentage Explained

Why body fat percentage tells you more than the number on the scale ever will.

When most people track their fitness progress, they focus on the number on the scale. But body weight alone is a poor indicator of health and fitness. Two people can weigh exactly the same, have the same BMI, and look completely different — because one carries significantly more muscle and less fat than the other. The missing metric is body fat percentage, which measures the proportion of your total body weight that consists of fat tissue. Understanding your body fat percentage gives you a far more accurate and useful picture of your health than weight or BMI alone.

What Is Body Fat Percentage?

Body fat percentage is calculated as: (total fat mass ÷ total body mass) × 100. For example, a person weighing 80 kg with 16 kg of body fat has a body fat percentage of 20%. The remaining 80% of their body weight consists of lean mass — muscle, bone, organs, water, and connective tissue.

Body fat is not simply stored energy. It plays several essential roles in the body, which is why a minimum level of "essential fat" is required for survival. Body fat insulates the body against cold, cushions and protects vital organs, participates in hormone production, enables the absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E and K), and provides a fuel reserve for periods of prolonged low-intensity activity or caloric restriction.

Essential Fat vs Storage Fat

Body fat is divided into two categories with very different functions:

Essential fat is stored in bone marrow, nerves, the brain, heart, lungs, liver, spleen, kidneys, intestines, and muscles. This fat is required for normal physiological function and cannot be eliminated without serious health consequences. Women require significantly more essential fat than men due to the demands of hormonal function and reproductive physiology.

Storage fat is the fat accumulated in adipose tissue throughout the body. It is further divided into subcutaneous fat (stored just beneath the skin) and visceral fat (stored around internal organs in the abdominal cavity). Subcutaneous fat is relatively benign in moderate amounts. Visceral fat, by contrast, is metabolically active and strongly associated with insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and inflammation.

How Body Fat Changes With Age

It is entirely normal for body fat percentage to increase gradually with age, even when body weight remains stable. This occurs because muscle mass naturally declines with age — a process called sarcopenia — while fat mass tends to increase. Between the ages of 30 and 70, the average adult loses approximately 3–8% of their muscle mass per decade without deliberate resistance training. This muscle loss is replaced by fat tissue, raising body fat percentage even in the absence of weight gain.

This means the healthy body fat range for a 60-year-old is somewhat higher than for a 25-year-old. It also underscores the importance of resistance training at every age — preserving muscle mass is one of the most powerful things you can do to maintain metabolic health, functional independence, and quality of life as you age.

Why Fat Distribution Matters

Where you carry fat is at least as important as how much you carry. Visceral fat — the fat stored deep in the abdominal cavity around organs such as the liver, pancreas, and intestines — is far more metabolically dangerous than subcutaneous fat. Visceral fat secretes inflammatory proteins and hormones that disrupt insulin signalling, raise blood pressure, elevate triglycerides, lower HDL (good) cholesterol, and promote atherosclerosis.

The classic "apple shape" (excess abdominal fat) is associated with higher cardiovascular risk than the "pear shape" (excess hip and thigh fat). Simple measurements such as waist circumference, waist-to-height ratio, and waist-to-hip ratio are useful proxies for visceral fat and can complement body fat percentage measurements.

How to Measure Body Fat Percentage

Several methods are available, ranging from highly accurate medical-grade tests to convenient at-home estimates:

DEXA Scan (Dual-Energy X-ray Absorptiometry) is the gold standard for body composition measurement. It differentiates between fat mass, lean mass, and bone mineral density with approximately 1–2% error. It is expensive (typically £100–300) and requires access to a specialist clinic but provides the most comprehensive picture of body composition.

Hydrostatic Weighing measures body density by comparing weight on land to weight submerged in water. Accuracy is comparable to DEXA but the process is cumbersome and not widely accessible.

Skinfold Calipers use measurements of skin thickness at specific anatomical sites to estimate subcutaneous fat. When performed by an experienced practitioner using validated equations, accuracy is within 3–4%. At-home use is less accurate due to variability in technique.

Bioelectrical Impedance Analysis (BIA) sends a small electrical current through the body and measures resistance to estimate fat mass. Smart scales using BIA are widely available and convenient for tracking trends over time, but can vary by 2–5% based on hydration levels, time of day, and recent food intake. Always measure under the same conditions for consistent results.

Tape Measure Method uses circumference measurements at the waist, hips, and neck to estimate body fat using standard formulas — including the US Army method, which our army body fat calculator uses. This method is free, accessible, and reasonably accurate when measurements are taken carefully.

How to Reduce Body Fat

Reducing body fat requires a consistent calorie deficit over time — consuming fewer calories than your body burns. The most effective approach combines a moderate calorie reduction with regular exercise, particularly a combination of cardiovascular exercise and resistance training. Cardio burns calories and improves cardiovascular fitness. Resistance training preserves (or builds) muscle mass, which is critical because muscle tissue burns more calories at rest than fat tissue.

Protein intake is particularly important during fat loss. Eating 1.6–2.4g of protein per kg of body weight preserves muscle mass during a calorie deficit, ensuring that weight lost comes primarily from fat rather than muscle.
