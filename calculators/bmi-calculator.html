Understanding Your BMI Result

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a screening tool that estimates whether your body weight is appropriate for your height. It is calculated by dividing your weight in kilograms by the square of your height in metres. While BMI is widely used by healthcare professionals worldwide, it has important limitations — it does not distinguish between fat and muscle, and does not account for where fat is distributed in the body.

For a more complete picture of your health, use BMI alongside body fat percentage, waist-to-height ratio, and your daily calorie needs. Read our full guide: What Is BMI and Why It Matters.

FAQs

What is a healthy BMI?

18.5–24.9 is considered healthy for most adults. However, athletes with high muscle mass may have a higher BMI while being perfectly healthy.

Is BMI accurate for everyone?

BMI is a population-level screening tool. It is less accurate for athletes, elderly people, pregnant women, and people of Asian descent (who tend to carry more risk at lower BMI values).

What should I do if my BMI is high?

Consult a healthcare professional. A high BMI indicates increased risk, but your doctor will consider other factors including blood pressure, blood sugar, cholesterol, fitness level and family history.
